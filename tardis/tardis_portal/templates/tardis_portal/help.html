OAGR Help

What is a Source?

Sources are individual ancient human remains, from which samples are taken to generate ancient human genome and microbiome data. If the location of the source is known, it is displayed on the OAGR map. Clicking on a Source title (either via the list of the map) will load a page that displays information about the Source, including provenance metadata. Any samples taken from the Source are listed below the provenance metadata, along with links to Datasets derived from the samples.

What is a Study?

Studies are groups of related Datasets. In many cases there will only be one Dataset per Study, but it is possible for a Study to have multiple Datasets. Clicking on the title of a Study will load a page that displays information about the Study on the left and links to the included Dataset(s) on the right.

What is a Dataset?

A Dataset is a group of related files and accompanying metadata. Clicking on the title of the Dataset will load a page that displays information about how the Dataset was generated. In cases where the files are generated from multiple bioinformatics pipelines, metadata about each pipeline appears separately and can be accessed using the tabs at the top of the dataset, or in some cases through a drop-down menu depending on the number of bioinformatics pipelines incorporated. The individual files appear on the right.

Why do some Datasets not have any related files?

In some cases, the metadata about a Dataset is published before the data files are available. Data files may be released at a later stage.

How do I download Datasets?

Clicking on the title of the Dataset will load a page that displays information about the Dataset and lists included data files on the right-hand side of the page. Files can be downloaded individually by clicking the download icon next to the file name. Selected files can be downloaded by clicking the checkbox next to the required files and clicking the ‘Download Selected Files’ button. The entire Dataset (including a text file containing metadata) can be downloaded by clicking the TAR download button.

OAGR can also show a list of direct download URLs for a batch download of files or datasets. Click the checkbox next to the required files and click ‘Show Download URLs’ button.

Can I reuse data in OAGR?

Data published through OAGR is made openly available under the Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International (CC BY 4.0) licence. Under this licence you are permitted to share (copy and redistribute in any medium or format) and adapt (remix, transform, and build on) the data and metadata for any purpose, including commercially, provided you:

  • attribute the creator/s of the data
  • link to the licence
  • indicate if changes were made

Where does the data come from?

Ancient human genome and microbiome data, including raw sequence and processed data, is generated from ancient samples studied at the Australian Centre for Ancient DNA, University of Adelaide, in collaboration with other research groups.

How do I cite something in OAGR?

Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs) will be minted for included Datasets and Studies to enable persistent identification and citation. The repository will display suggested citations, which you can use to cite and link to a Dataset or Study, for example:

Smith, Jane; Smart, John (2014): Title of collection. Online Ancient Genome Repository. Collection. http://dx.doi.org/10.4225/38/123456789

How do I view files in a genome browser?

Files with .vcf or .bam extension can be viewed in the external UCSC genome browser. Simply click the ‘Open in genome browser’ button next to a listed .vcf or .bam file. The UCSC Genome Browser opens in new window/tab.

Can I upload my data?

OAGR is currently limited to data generated from ancient samples studied at the Australian Centre for Ancient DNA in collaboration with other research groups, accordance with the Repository Guidelines. Individuals and groups interested in collaborations should contact ACAD.

I can’t open TAR files!

You may require tools to support TAR files. Open source operating systems often include tools such as gzip and bzip2 but other third party tools are available for other platforms.

Which browsers are recommended?

OAGR can be accessed on all internet browsers but for the best experience it is recommended that you use either Chrome or Firefox.
